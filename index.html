🚀 Getting Started

Before any work begins, every project needs a solid foundation. This section guides you through initiation — the most critical phase.

Key Takeaways

  • Every project must begin with a clear Project Charter defining scope, objectives and stakeholders
  • The initiation phase prevents scope creep — the number one cause of project failure (Kerzner, 2022)
  • Small charities should agree a Scope Definition document with their client or board before proceeding
  • The SMART framework ensures objectives are measurable and achievable from day one
Phase 1

Project Initiation Essentials

The Project Management Institute (PMI, 2021) defines initiation as the process of authorising a new project or phase. For small teams, this means three key documents.

📄

Project Charter

A one-page document that formally authorises the project. It names the project manager, defines high-level scope, budget and timeline, and lists key stakeholders.

PMI (2021) PMBOK Guide, 7th edn.

🎯

SMART Objectives

All project goals must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Time-bound. Vague goals lead to disagreement, delays and unmet expectations (Larson & Gray, 2021).

Larson & Gray (2021), p. 82

📐

Scope Definition

Clearly documents what the project WILL and WON'T deliver. Without this, scope creep — gradual expansion of requirements — will drain resources and delay completion.

Kerzner (2022), p. 144

👥 Teams

Understanding how teams form, develop and perform is fundamental to every project's success — especially in volunteer-led Irish organisations.

Key Takeaways

  • Teams go through predictable stages of development (Tuckman, 1965) — understanding this prevents unnecessary conflict
  • Belbin's Team Roles (2010) ensure a balanced skill mix across nine complementary roles
  • Volunteer teams in Irish charities face unique challenges around commitment, turnover and motivation
  • Psychological safety — not just skill — determines whether teams perform at their best (Edmondson, 1999)
Team Development

Tuckman's Five Stages of Team Development

Tuckman (1965) identified four sequential stages of team development, later adding a fifth (Tuckman & Jensen, 1977). Every team progresses through these — the manager's role is to accelerate positive transitions.

Stage 1

Forming

Polite, cautious. Team members getting to know each other and establishing norms.

Stage 2

Storming

Conflict emerges. Roles challenged, frustrations voiced. Most teams get stuck here.

Stage 3

Norming

Agreement on working methods. Trust builds, collaboration improves.

Stage 4

Performing

High performance. Team is self-directed and fully focused on project goals.

Stage 5

Adjourning

Project ends. Team disbands — managed well, this becomes a source of pride.

Source: Tuckman, B.W. (1965) 'Developmental sequence in small groups', Psychological Bulletin, 63(6), pp. 384–399. Tuckman, B.W. and Jensen, M.A.C. (1977) 'Stages of small-group development revisited', Group & Organization Studies, 2(4), pp. 419–427.

Team Composition

Belbin's Nine Team Roles

Belbin (2010) identified nine roles that successful teams require. No single person can fulfil all nine — understanding your team's profile allows you to identify gaps and manage them proactively.

💡

Plant

Creative problem-solver who generates original ideas. Can be poor at communicating them.

🔍

Monitor Evaluator

Analytical and objective. Evaluates options carefully before making decisions.

🌐

Resource Investigator

Explores opportunities and develops contacts. Enthusiastic but can lose momentum.

🤝

Team Worker

Cooperative and diplomatic. Helps team gel and averts friction — especially valuable in Irish charity contexts.

⚡

Shaper

Challenges, drives and finds ways around obstacles. Can be provocative under pressure.

🔧

Implementer

Turns ideas into practical action plans. Disciplined and reliable, but inflexible.

Source: Belbin, R.M. (2010) Team Roles at Work. 2nd edn. Oxford: Routledge.

Irish Context

Managing Volunteer Teams

🏠

Flexible Scheduling

Volunteers have primary jobs and family commitments. Effective Irish charity PMs build schedules around volunteer availability, not the other way around (Volunteer Ireland, 2022).

🏆

Recognition & Motivation

Intrinsic motivation drives volunteers. Regular acknowledgement, progress updates and celebrating milestones dramatically reduces dropout rates (Larson & Gray, 2021).

📜

Clear Role Descriptions

RACI charts (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) prevent confusion in volunteer teams where roles often blur (PMI, 2021).

🤝 Stakeholder Management

Identifying and engaging the right people at the right time is one of the most critical success factors in any project (PMI, 2021).

Key Takeaways

  • Freeman (1984) defines a stakeholder as "any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organisation's purpose"
  • The Power/Interest matrix helps prioritise where to invest communication effort
  • A RACI chart prevents responsibility gaps on complex projects
  • In Irish charities, donors and funding bodies are high-power stakeholders often overlooked in planning
Framework

The Power / Interest Matrix

Originally developed by Johnson & Scholes (1993) and widely applied in PM practice (PMI, 2021), this matrix guides your communication strategy.

POWER ↑
Keep Satisfied

High power, low interest — keep informed but don't overwhelm.

Manage Closely

High power, high interest — your primary stakeholders. Engage actively.

Monitor Only

Low power, low interest — monitor but minimal effort.

Keep Informed

Low power, high interest — keep them informed, they can become advocates.

LOW INTEREST
HIGH INTEREST →

Source: Johnson, G. and Scholes, K. (1993) Exploring Corporate Strategy. 3rd edn. Hemel Hempstead: Prentice Hall. Applied via PMI (2021).

🗺️ Strategy & Planning

A project without a plan is just a wish. This section covers business cases, roadmaps and the planning tools every small team needs.

Key Takeaways

  • A business case justifies the investment in the project — without it, stakeholders won't commit (Axelos, 2017)
  • Work Breakdown Structures (WBS) break complex projects into manageable tasks
  • SWOT analysis grounds strategy in current reality before committing resources
  • For small Irish orgs, a simple one-page roadmap is often more effective than complex Gantt charts
Planning Tools

Essential Strategy & Planning Frameworks

💼

Business Case

Documents why the project is needed, what it will cost, expected benefits and risks. Required for board/funder approval. PRINCE2 (Axelos, 2017) treats the business case as the central document throughout a project.

Axelos (2017) PRINCE2, 6th edn.

🌳

Work Breakdown Structure

Hierarchically decomposes project deliverables into smaller, manageable work packages. The WBS is the foundation of the project schedule and resource plan (PMI, 2021).

PMI (2021), p. 156

📅

Gantt Chart

Visual timeline showing tasks, durations and dependencies. Free tools like Microsoft Planner or Trello make basic Gantt charts accessible to any Irish charity (Larson & Gray, 2021).

Larson & Gray (2021), p. 201

🔭

SWOT Analysis

Identifies Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats. For small Irish businesses, this grounds strategic planning in honest current-state assessment before committing to a project direction.

Johnson et al. (2017)

⚙️ Implementation

The execution phase is where plans become reality. Effective implementation requires clear delegation, progress tracking and responsive decision-making.

Key Takeaways

  • Implementation is the longest and most resource-intensive project phase (Kerzner, 2022)
  • Status reports, issued weekly, keep all stakeholders aligned and surface issues early
  • Change control processes prevent unplanned scope changes derailing delivery
  • Daily stand-ups (15 minutes) from Agile practice work just as well for small charity teams
Execution

From Plan to Delivery

Week 1–2: Kick-off and Role Assignment

Hold a formal kick-off meeting. Confirm RACI chart, agree communication cadence and distribute the project plan to all team members (PMI, 2021).

Week 2 onwards: Weekly Status Reports

A simple one-page status report covering: what was done, what's next, risks/issues and any decisions needed from leadership (Larson & Gray, 2021).

Mid-project: Milestone Review

At every major milestone, hold a formal review against the original plan. Document variances and agree corrective actions. Feed learning back into the risk register.

Change Control: Any Time

Any change to scope, budget or timeline must go through a formal change request. For small teams, a simple email approval from the sponsor or board is sufficient (Axelos, 2017).

Close-out: Lessons Learned

A lessons learned session within two weeks of project close captures knowledge for future projects. This is an explicit requirement of the PMBOK Guide (PMI, 2021).

🛡️ Risk Management

Risk management is not about avoiding failure — it is about managing the inevitable. For Irish charities with stretched resources, a structured approach to risk is non-negotiable.

Key Takeaways

  • ISO 31000 (2018) defines risk as "the effect of uncertainty on objectives" — both threats and opportunities
  • A Risk Register is a living document — it must be reviewed and updated regularly throughout the project
  • The 5×5 Risk Matrix (likelihood × impact) is the standard tool for prioritising risks (PMI, 2021)
  • Without a risk register, small teams are especially vulnerable to unforeseen disruptions (Murray-Webster & Hillson, 2021)
Framework

The Five-Step Risk Process

🔎

1. Identify

Brainstorm all potential threats and opportunities using structured workshops, checklists and historical data from similar projects (ISO 31000, 2018).

📊

2. Assess

Score each risk on Likelihood (1–5) and Impact (1–5). Risk Score = L × I. This produces a priority ranking for your team's limited attention and resources (PMI, 2021).

🛠️

3. Plan Response

For each high-priority risk, assign one of four strategies: Avoid, Transfer (e.g. insurance), Mitigate (reduce likelihood/impact), or Accept (Murray-Webster & Hillson, 2021).

👁️

4. Monitor

Review the risk register at every project meeting. Add new risks as they emerge. Track whether mitigation actions are being completed on time (Kerzner, 2022).

⚡

5. Respond

When a risk event occurs, execute the pre-planned response. Debrief afterwards to capture learning. Update the register with actual impact data for future reference.

A community-led project to convert three underused urban lots in Cork City into accessible green community spaces. The project faced significant challenges with local council approval processes, volunteer availability and unexpected ground contamination on site 2.

💡 Leadership

Effective project leadership is the single most cited differentiator between project success and failure. For small Irish organisations, leadership style shapes everything.

Key Takeaways

  • Transformational leaders inspire change; transactional leaders manage compliance. Both have roles in project work (Bass & Avolio, 1994)
  • Servant leadership — prioritising team needs — is especially effective in volunteer-led Irish charities (Greenleaf, 1977)
  • Emotional Intelligence (EI) is a stronger predictor of project leadership effectiveness than IQ (Goleman, 1996)
  • Kotter's (2012) 8-step model remains the most cited framework for leading change in organisations

Emotional Intelligence

Goleman's Five Dimensions of EI

🧠

Self-Awareness

Recognising your own emotions and how they affect your behaviour and decisions. The foundation of all other EI dimensions (Goleman, 1996).

⚖️

Self-Regulation

Managing disruptive emotions. Project managers who self-regulate create psychological safety for the entire team (Edmondson, 1999).

🔥

Motivation

Drive to achieve beyond expectations. Intrinsically motivated leaders inspire the same in their teams — critical for volunteer-led projects.

💚

Empathy

Understanding others' perspectives. Especially critical when managing diverse volunteer teams with different life pressures and motivations.

🤝

Social Skills

Managing relationships and building networks. Project managers with strong social skills are better at stakeholder management and conflict resolution.

Source: Goleman, D. (1996) Emotional Intelligence. London: Bloomsbury.

🌱 Ethics & Sustainability

Project management is not just about timelines and budgets — it is about the long-term impact of your work on people, communities and the planet.

Key Takeaways

  • The PMI Code of Ethics (2006) identifies four core values: Responsibility, Respect, Fairness and Honesty
  • The UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) provide a globally recognised framework for embedding sustainability into any project
  • Irish charities are legally required to comply with the Charities Regulator Governance Code, which includes ethics provisions (Charities Regulator Ireland, 2021)
  • Sustainable project management reduces long-term operational costs and strengthens funder confidence
PMI Code of Ethics

Four Core Ethical Values

🤝 Responsibility

We make decisions based on the best interests of society, public safety and the environment. We accept accountability for our own and our team's decisions (PMI Code of Ethics, 2006).

🌟 Respect

We respect ourselves, others and the resources entrusted to us. This includes time, money, reputation and the environment we leave behind for future generations.

⚖️ Fairness

We make decisions impartially and objectively. We proactively disclose conflicts of interest — particularly important for charity trustees and project sponsors.

🔍 Honesty

We create an environment in which truth is valued. We do not engage in or permit deceptive reporting, data manipulation or unethical stakeholder communication.

Source: Project Management Institute (2006) PMI Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct. Newtown Square, PA: PMI.

⚖️ Governance

Good governance is the structural foundation that allows small Irish organisations to deliver projects with accountability, transparency and public trust.

Key Takeaways

  • The Charities Regulator Ireland (2021) Governance Code sets out five principles all Irish charities must follow
  • Governance is not bureaucracy — it is the framework that protects your organisation, your funders and the people you serve
  • Project governance defines decision-making authority, escalation paths and reporting obligations
  • GDPR compliance (Data Protection Commission Ireland, 2018) is an essential governance requirement for all organisations handling personal data
Irish Governance Code

Five Principles of Good Governance

The Charities Regulator Ireland (2021) Governance Code is a practical framework for charities and voluntary organisations. It is structured around five principles that align directly with good project management practice.

🎯

Principle 1: Lead

The board leads the organisation and is responsible for directing its affairs. For project managers, this means understanding the chain of authority before initiating any project (Charities Regulator Ireland, 2021).

📋

Principle 2: Plan & Deliver

Organisations must have strategic plans and must deliver on them. Project plans should be directly traceable to the organisation's strategic objectives.

💰

Principle 3: Be Transparent & Accountable

Annual reporting, financial transparency and public accountability are required. Project budgets and outcomes should be documented and available to key stakeholders.

🛡️

Principle 4: Be Ethical

Conflicts of interest must be declared and managed. In project management, this means declaring any financial relationship with suppliers or contractors at the outset.

⚙️

Principle 5: Be Effective

Organisations must evaluate their own performance. Post-project lessons learned reviews directly support this principle by generating evidence of effectiveness.

Source: Charities Regulator Ireland (2021) Governance Code for Community, Voluntary and Charitable Organisations. Available at: https://www.charitiesregulator.ie

Project Governance Structure

Governance at the Project Level

Board / Trustees

Strategic oversight. Approve major projects, review annual performance and ensure charitable purpose is upheld. Highest level of governance authority.

Senior Management / CEO

Operational accountability. Sponsors projects, approves budgets within delegated authority limits, escalates material risks to the board.

Project Manager

Day-to-day delivery. Manages scope, schedule, budget and team. Reports to senior management on project status and escalates issues through defined channels.

Project Team / Volunteers

Deliver work packages as defined in the RACI chart. Responsible for quality and timely completion. Raise risks and issues to the project manager immediately.

GDPR

Data Protection in Irish Projects

🔒

GDPR Compliance

Any project collecting personal data from beneficiaries, donors or volunteers must comply with GDPR (Data Protection Commission Ireland, 2018). This includes lawful basis, consent, data minimisation and right to erasure.

📧

Communications Data

Contact lists, survey responses and volunteer records are personal data. They must be stored securely, not shared without consent, and deleted when no longer required.

📝

Privacy Policy

Any public-facing project activity (website, events, fundraising) requires a clear Privacy Policy and Data Protection Officer designation for larger organisations.

Source: Data Protection Commission Ireland (2018) Guide to GDPR for Organisations. Available at: https://www.dataprotection.ie

🔄 Methodologies

Choosing the right project management methodology for your organisation type, project complexity and team structure is one of the most important decisions a PM can make.

Key Takeaways

  • There is no single "best" methodology — the right choice depends on project complexity, team experience and stakeholder requirements (Larson & Gray, 2021)
  • PRINCE2 is widely used in Irish public sector and larger charities due to its governance structure (Axelos, 2017)
  • Agile is increasingly adopted by small Irish teams for its flexibility and iterative approach (Beck et al., 2001)
  • Hybrid approaches — combining Waterfall planning with Agile execution — are often most practical for small organisations
Comparison

Five Key Methodologies Compared

MethodologyBest ForApproachSmall Org FitIrish Use
WaterfallFixed-scope projects with clear requirementsSequential phases, each fully complete before the next beginsMediumConstruction, IT infrastructure
AgileProjects with evolving requirementsIterative sprints; deliver working product regularlyHighSoftware, service design
PRINCE2Complex, governance-heavy projectsStage-gated; strong documentation and board involvementMediumGovernment, HSE, larger charities
LeanProcess improvement; waste reductionEliminate waste, maximise value deliveryHighHealthcare, food banks, logistics

Decision Tool

Which Methodology Should I Use?

Are requirements fully known upfront?

✅ Yes → Waterfall or PRINCE2
❌ No → Agile or Hybrid

Is governance & accountability critical?

✅ Yes → PRINCE2
❌ Less critical → Agile or Lean

Is your team small (<10 people)?

✅ Yes → Agile, Lean or Hybrid
❌ Larger team → PRINCE2 or Waterfall

Is this a process improvement project?

✅ Yes → Lean
❌ No → assess scope and team size first

"The methodology should serve the project — not the other way around."

🤖 AI in Projects

Artificial intelligence tools are transforming how small teams plan, communicate and report. This section provides practical guidance on using AI responsibly in an Irish charity context.

Key Takeaways

  • The PMI Pulse of the Profession (2023) found that 21% of project managers now use AI tools regularly in their work
  • AI excels at routine tasks: meeting summaries, draft reports, risk brainstorming and scheduling — freeing PMs for high-value decisions
  • GDPR compliance is essential before using AI tools on any personal or beneficiary data in an Irish context (DPC Ireland, 2018)
  • The most effective PMs use AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement for professional judgement (Pew Research Center, 2023)

Ethical Considerations

Using AI Responsibly in Charity Work

🔐

Data Privacy (GDPR)

Never input personal data about beneficiaries, donors or staff into external AI tools without checking the provider's GDPR compliance. Irish organisations are bound by EU data protection law (DPC Ireland, 2018).

⚠️

Bias & Accuracy

AI outputs can reflect biases in training data and contain factual errors. All AI-generated content must be verified against credible sources before use in project documentation (Pew Research Center, 2023).

👤

Human Oversight

AI should augment professional judgement, not replace it. Major project decisions — scope changes, budget approvals, stakeholder communications — must always involve human review.

📚 Real-World Case Studies

Theory becomes meaningful through application. These case studies show how project management tools and frameworks work in real Irish and international contexts.

Case Study 1: Bríd's Community Kitchen

Fictional · Irish Charity
Sector: Food Security / Social EnterpriseLocation: Dublin 8Team: 1 co-ordinator, 12 volunteersDuration: 8 months

Bríd's Community Kitchen was established to provide daily hot meals to food-insecure adults in the Liberties area of Dublin. Following a successful pilot, the organisation received €35,000 in funding from the Community Foundation Ireland to scale operations and formalise its project management approach.

Project Management Approach Applied:

  • ✓ Methodology: Hybrid (Waterfall planning + Agile weekly sprint reviews)
  • ✓ Governance: Charities Regulator Governance Code adopted as operating framework
  • ✓ Risk Management: Full risk register maintained; volunteer dropout risk mitigated by 30% buffer recruitment
  • ✓ Stakeholder Engagement: Power/Interest matrix applied; Dublin City Council and Dept. of Social Protection managed as high-power stakeholders
  • ✓ SDG Alignment: Project explicitly mapped to SDG 2 (Zero Hunger) and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities) in funder reporting
Outcomes (8 months)

480 meals served per week (up from 120 in pilot) · Volunteer retention 91% · €4,200 under budget · Follow-on funding of €50,000 secured · Cited by Community Foundation Ireland as a model governance example for small food charities.

Case Study 2: TechFirst SME — Cloud Migration Project

Fictional · Irish SME
Sector: Accountancy ServicesLocation: Galway CityTeam: 6 staffDuration: 4 months

TechFirst, a 14-person accountancy firm in Galway, undertook a full migration from paper-based and local-server systems to a cloud-based platform (Microsoft 365 + SharePoint). The project involved significant change management challenges, data security requirements and staff resistance to technology change.

Key Challenges & PM Responses:

ChallengePM ResponseFramework Used
Staff resistance to new systemKotter's 8-step change model applied; early wins communicated widelyKotter (2012)
GDPR risk during data migrationData Protection Impact Assessment completed; DPC notifiedDPC Ireland (2018)
Scope creep (IT dept adding features)Formal change control process implemented from week 2Axelos PRINCE2 (2017)
Timeline slippage (2 weeks)Crashing: additional IT resource brought in at €1,800Kerzner (2022)
Outcomes

Migration completed 2 weeks late but within budget. All 14 staff fully trained. Client data 100% migrated with zero loss. GDPR audit passed. Estimated annual saving: €12,000 in reduced paper/printing costs and IT maintenance.
